About the Mission

The Kepler Space Telescope, launched by NASA in 2009, revolutionized our understanding of planets beyond our solar system. Named after the 17th-century astronomer Johannes Kepler, this space observatory was designed with a singular purpose: to discover Earth-size planets orbiting other stars.

The Legacy

During its nine-year mission, Kepler surveyed just one patch of sky in the northern hemisphere, determining that planets are common throughout the Milky Way galaxy. The most recent analysis of Kepler's discoveries shows that 20-50% of the stars visible in the night sky are likely to have small, possibly rocky planets similar in size to Earth within their habitable zones.

Understanding Kepler Data

Light Curves

Light curves are graphs showing a star's brightness over time. When a planet passes in front of its star (called a transit), it creates a distinctive dip in the star's brightness. These periodic dips help us detect and study exoplanets.

Data Collection

Kepler monitored over 150,000 stars simultaneously, measuring their brightness every 30 minutes. This created detailed light curves that reveal not only planets but also other stellar phenomena.

Transit Method

The depth of the brightness dip indicates the planet's size relative to its star. The timing between dips reveals the planet's orbital period, while the duration provides clues about its orbital distance.
